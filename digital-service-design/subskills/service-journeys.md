# Service Journeys Subskill

## Purpose

Map the full resident experience of a service from initial need through completion, follow-up, and possible recovery. Use this subskill to identify gaps between what residents need and how the County currently presents or delivers the service.

## When to use

Use for services that involve multiple pages, forms, departments, channels, documents, payments, appointments, inspections, approvals, or follow-up messages.

## Journey framing

Start by defining:

- Resident goal
- Resident situation or trigger
- Service outcome
- Channels involved
- County teams or systems involved
- Required resident actions
- Required County actions
- Hand-off points
- Completion point
- Failure or recovery paths

## Default journey stages

1. **Recognize need** — The resident realizes they need to apply, pay, report, request, register, appeal, contact, or check eligibility.
2. **Find service** — The resident searches, navigates, follows a link, receives a notice, or contacts staff.
3. **Evaluate fit** — The resident determines whether this is the right service and whether they are eligible.
4. **Prepare** — The resident gathers documents, information, account access, payment method, or authorization.
5. **Start** — The resident opens the form, calls, emails, visits, signs in, or begins the transaction.
6. **Complete** — The resident submits information, pays, schedules, signs, uploads, or confirms.
7. **Receive confirmation** — The resident gets a receipt, case number, tracking number, email, or staff confirmation.
8. **Wait or act** — The resident waits for review, responds to requests, attends an appointment, or completes additional steps.
9. **Resolve** — The service is approved, denied, fulfilled, closed, renewed, escalated, appealed, or withdrawn.
10. **Recover** — The resident handles an error, missing item, denial, missed deadline, payment problem, or changed circumstance.

## Analysis prompts

Ask:

- Where does the resident think the service starts and ends?
- What does the County assume the resident already knows?
- Where are eligibility, cost, deadlines, documents, and processing times introduced?
- Where does the resident switch channels?
- What must the resident repeat?
- What happens after submission?
- What proof does the resident receive?
- Where can the resident check status?
- Where do residents most often abandon, call, email, or submit incomplete requests?
- What happens when the service cannot proceed?

## Quality criteria

A service journey is useful when it:

- Uses resident-facing language.
- Includes all channels, not only the website.
- Identifies handoffs and waiting periods.
- Shows where the resident receives proof or status.
- Includes common failure states and recovery paths.
- Produces concrete content or workflow improvements.
