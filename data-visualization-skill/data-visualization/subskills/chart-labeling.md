# Chart Labeling Subskill

## Purpose
Improve chart titles, subtitles, axes, legends, annotations, captions, source notes, and units so the chart can be understood quickly and accurately.

## Use when
- A chart title is vague, such as "Results" or "Program Data."
- Axis labels, units, dates, or geography are missing.
- A legend forces unnecessary back-and-forth reading.
- The chart needs a caption, source note, or caveat.

## Labeling standards

### Title
Use an informative title that states the subject and, when appropriate, the key takeaway.

Weak: "Permit Applications"
Better: "Permit applications increased from 2021 to 2024"

### Subtitle
Use the subtitle for scope, geography, date range, population, or method.

Example: "Countywide monthly applications, January 2021 through December 2024"

### Axis labels
Include units and time period. Avoid unexplained abbreviations. Use plain words.

Examples:
- "Applications received"
- "Percent of inspections completed on time"
- "Fiscal year"

### Legends
Prefer direct labels on chart elements when space allows. If using a legend, place it close to the data and use clear category names.

### Annotations
Use annotations sparingly to explain major changes, policy changes, data gaps, or unusual values. Do not annotate every point.

### Captions and source notes
Include source, date pulled or updated, and caveats where needed.

Example: "Source: Department records, updated March 2026. Data excludes withdrawn applications."

## Review checklist

- Does the title explain what the chart is about?
- Are units, dates, geography, and population clear?
- Can the chart be understood without reading surrounding paragraphs?
- Are abbreviations defined?
- Are caveats close to the visual?
- Does the labeling avoid overstating causation, precision, or certainty?
