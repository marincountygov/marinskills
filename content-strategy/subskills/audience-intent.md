# Subskill: Audience and Intent

## Purpose

Use this subskill to identify who the content serves, what they are trying to do, and what questions or barriers shape the content strategy.

## Core rule

Audience definition must be specific enough to guide decisions. “The public,” “residents,” “staff,” or “users” is usually too broad unless segmented further.

## Audience model

For each audience, define:

- Who they are.
- Their relationship to the content.
- Their task or decision.
- Their context of use.
- Their likely knowledge level.
- Their urgency or emotional state.
- Barriers they may face.
- What they need next.

## Primary vs. secondary audiences

### Primary audience

The group whose task determines the content structure. Optimize for them first.

### Secondary audience

Groups that may also use the content but should not distort the primary purpose. Support them through links, secondary sections, or related content where appropriate.

### Internal audience

Staff, partners, vendors, or decision-makers who may need process or governance information. Do not mix internal process details into public-facing pages unless users need them.

## Intent categories

Classify intent into one dominant category.

### Do

Users need to complete an action.

Examples:

- Apply for a permit.
- Pay a bill.
- Register for a class.
- Report a problem.

Strategy:

- Prioritize clear steps, requirements, deadlines, fees, forms, and contact paths.

### Know

Users need to understand something before deciding or acting.

Examples:

- Learn eligibility rules.
- Understand a process.
- Compare options.

Strategy:

- Provide concise explanations and clear next steps.

### Find

Users need to locate a person, service, place, document, event, or status.

Strategy:

- Prioritize labels, metadata, filters, search terms, and navigation pathways.

### Decide

Users need to choose among options.

Strategy:

- Compare criteria, eligibility, consequences, timing, and tradeoffs.

### Comply

Users need to meet a requirement.

Strategy:

- State obligations, deadlines, authority, evidence, penalties if relevant, and how to comply.

### Participate

Users need to attend, comment, provide feedback, volunteer, or engage in a public process.

Strategy:

- State what participation means, when and how to participate, what happens afterward, and where outcomes will be posted.

## User questions

Translate content needs into user questions. Good user questions are concrete.

Examples:

- Am I eligible?
- What do I need before I start?
- How much does it cost?
- How long will it take?
- What happens after I submit?
- Who do I contact if something goes wrong?
- Is this still current?

## Avoid organization-first framing

Weak framing:

- “We need a page about the department’s program.”

Better framing:

- “Residents need to know whether they qualify, how to apply, and what happens after they apply.”

## Quality checks

A good audience-intent analysis:

- Names a specific audience segment.
- Defines a task, not just a topic.
- Separates public needs from internal needs.
- Identifies what the user already knows or does not know.
- Identifies what the user needs next.
- Gives enough detail to shape title, structure, content type, metadata, and success measures.
