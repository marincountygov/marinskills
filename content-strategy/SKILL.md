# Content Strategy Skill

## Purpose

Use this skill to define why content exists, who it serves, where it belongs, how long it should live, and how success is measured.

This skill is for planning, evaluating, structuring, governing, and improving content before, during, and after publication. It should be used for websites, intranet pages, service pages, campaign pages, landing pages, forms, guides, announcements, newsletters, social content, downloadable documents, knowledge-base articles, and other public- or staff-facing content.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when the user asks to:

- Create or revise a content strategy.
- Decide whether content should exist, be merged, archived, redirected, or rewritten.
- Define the audience, intent, owner, purpose, lifecycle, placement, metadata, taxonomy, or success measures for content.
- Plan a content inventory, content audit, migration, redesign, editorial calendar, governance model, or publication workflow.
- Review a page, section, content type, campaign, or document library for strategic fit.
- Convert vague content requests into clear user-centered publication requirements.

## When not to use this skill

Do not use this skill as a substitute for:

- Plain-language editing.
- Accessibility review.
- Visual design or brand review.
- Legal, records-retention, privacy, or policy determinations.
- Technical SEO implementation.
- Analytics instrumentation or dashboard engineering.

Route those tasks to the appropriate separate skills when available. This skill may identify that those reviews are needed, but it should not perform them unless the user explicitly asks and the required source material is available.

## Core operating principles

Content should exist only when it serves a clear user need and an accountable organizational purpose.

Every content item should have:

- A defined audience.
- A primary user intent.
- A clear page or asset purpose.
- A known owner.
- A lifecycle state.
- A planned location in the information architecture.
- Appropriate metadata and taxonomy.
- Measurable success criteria.
- A review, update, archival, or retirement plan.

Prefer fewer, better-maintained content items over fragmented, duplicated, or ownerless content.

## Required workflow

When using this skill, follow this sequence unless the user asks for a narrower task.

1. **Clarify the content object**
   - Identify whether the work concerns a page, section, document, campaign, calendar, content type, taxonomy, or audit set.
   - Identify whether it is new content, existing content, migrated content, or content under review.

2. **Define audience and intent**
   - Use `subskills/audience-intent.md`.
   - Identify the primary audience, secondary audiences, user tasks, user questions, and decision points.

3. **Define page or asset purpose**
   - Use `subskills/page-purpose.md`.
   - State what the content must help users do and what organizational outcome it supports.

4. **Classify content type**
   - Use `subskills/content-types.md`.
   - Choose the most appropriate content type and avoid using a content type for the wrong job.

5. **Determine placement and metadata**
   - Use `subskills/metadata-taxonomy.md`.
   - Identify where the content belongs, how it should be tagged, and what relationships it has to other content.

6. **Define lifecycle and governance**
   - Use `subskills/content-lifecycle.md`.
   - Assign ownership, review cadence, expiration triggers, archival rules, and maintenance requirements.

7. **Plan publication and maintenance**
   - Use `subskills/editorial-calendar.md` when scheduling is relevant.
   - Identify dependencies, review steps, publication dates, refresh dates, and campaign windows.

8. **Audit or measure existing content**
   - Use `subskills/content-audit.md` when reviewing current content.
   - Evaluate usefulness, accuracy, duplication, findability, performance, risk, and maintenance burden.

9. **Define success measures**
   - Choose metrics that reflect whether the content helped users complete the intended task.
   - Use outcome measures when possible, not only traffic volume.

## Standard outputs

Use the output format that best fits the request.

### Content strategy brief

Use for new content, major rewrites, migrations, or campaigns.

Include:

- Content title or working title.
- Content owner.
- Primary audience.
- Secondary audiences.
- User intent.
- Page or asset purpose.
- Content type.
- Recommended location.
- Related content.
- Metadata and taxonomy.
- Lifecycle state.
- Review cadence.
- Success measures.
- Risks, dependencies, or open questions.

### Page purpose statement

Use for individual pages or assets.

Format:

> This page exists to help [audience] [complete task or answer question] so they can [user outcome], while supporting [organizational purpose].

### Content decision recommendation

Use when evaluating existing content.

Choose one:

- Keep.
- Revise.
- Merge.
- Split.
- Move.
- Redirect.
- Archive.
- Delete.

Include the rationale, required actions, owner, and target date when known.

### Content lifecycle plan

Use for content that changes over time.

Include:

- Owner.
- Source of truth.
- Review cadence.
- Last reviewed date.
- Next review date.
- Expiration or sunset trigger.
- Archival disposition.
- Related records, policy, or legal review needs if applicable.

### Editorial calendar entry

Use for scheduled content.

Include:

- Item title.
- Content type.
- Audience.
- Channel.
- Owner.
- Draft due date.
- Review due date.
- Publish date.
- Unpublish or refresh date.
- Dependencies.
- Success measure.

## Decision rules

### Create content only when the answer is yes

Before recommending new content, confirm:

- There is a real user need.
- The audience is identifiable.
- The content has a responsible owner.
- The content has a maintainable source of truth.
- The content has a defined location and lifecycle.
- Similar content does not already meet the need.

If these are not true, recommend a different action, such as improving an existing page, adding metadata, redirecting users, or removing outdated content.

### Prefer task-based organization

Organize content around what users need to do, not around internal department structures, unless the user explicitly needs organizational or governance content.

### Use one primary purpose per page

A page may support multiple related tasks, but it should have one primary purpose. If a page has competing purposes or unrelated audiences, recommend splitting, restructuring, or creating clearer pathways.

### Treat ownership as required

Do not mark content as ready to publish unless ownership, maintenance responsibility, and review cadence are defined.

### Separate permanent, seasonal, and time-limited content

Permanent service content, seasonal campaign content, news, events, alerts, and policy updates need different lifecycle rules.

### Measure success by task completion

Do not treat page views as proof of success by themselves. Page views may indicate demand, but success should be tied to user task completion, reduced confusion, reduced support burden, appropriate conversions, or improved findability.

## Quality checklist

Before finalizing a content strategy output, verify:

- The audience is specific.
- The user intent is stated as a task or question.
- The content purpose is not merely “provide information.”
- The content type matches the purpose.
- The recommended location is justified.
- Duplicate or overlapping content has been identified.
- Ownership and lifecycle are assigned or flagged as unresolved.
- Success measures are meaningful.
- Open questions are explicit.

## Style for responses

Be direct and operational. Provide recommendations, not abstract theory. Use tables when comparing options, auditing content, or building calendars. Use concise headings and decision labels.

When information is missing, proceed with reasonable assumptions and clearly mark them. Do not block the work with excessive clarification questions.
