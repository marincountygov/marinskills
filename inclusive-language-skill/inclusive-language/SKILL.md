# Inclusive Language Skill

## Purpose
Support respectful, culturally competent, trauma-informed, disability-aware, age-aware, gender-aware, and resident-centered language.

Use this skill when creating, reviewing, editing, or advising on public-facing or internal content that refers to people, communities, identities, life circumstances, health, safety, services, eligibility, enforcement, or harm.

## Core operating principles

1. Put people and resident needs first.
   - Use language that helps people understand services, rights, responsibilities, risks, and next steps.
   - Avoid language that defines people only by a condition, status, incident, diagnosis, income level, case type, or interaction with government.

2. Be specific, accurate, and relevant.
   - Name characteristics only when they are necessary for the content purpose.
   - Do not add identity details, assumptions, or descriptors that are not needed for the service, notice, analysis, or call to action.

3. Prefer plain, direct, non-stigmatizing language.
   - Replace labels, euphemisms, bureaucratic phrasing, stereotypes, and blame-focused wording.
   - Use active, concrete language without assigning fault unless fault is legally established and relevant.

4. Preserve self-identification and legally required language.
   - Do not override how a person, community, or group identifies itself.
   - Keep statutory, regulatory, court, emergency, public health, or program terms when required, but explain them plainly.

5. Reduce harm in sensitive contexts.
   - Use trauma-informed framing when content involves violence, disasters, loss, enforcement, illness, disability, benefits, housing instability, abuse, death, or public safety.
   - Avoid graphic, sensational, moralizing, or fear-amplifying language unless necessary for safety.

6. Distinguish editorial guidance from specialist review.
   - Escalate legal, HR, civil rights, privacy, clinical, behavioral health, child welfare, law enforcement, and emergency communications questions to the appropriate subject-matter reviewer.
   - Do not invent policy positions, eligibility rules, protected-class interpretations, or legal conclusions.

## When to use subskills

Use the most relevant subskill based on the content context:

- `subskills/disability-language.md` for disability, access, functional needs, assistive technology, accommodations, and ableist wording.
- `subskills/race-ethnicity-national-origin.md` for race, ethnicity, nationality, immigration, language access, tribal communities, and cultural identifiers.
- `subskills/gender-family-identity.md` for gender identity, pronouns, sexual orientation, marital status, pregnancy, parenting, families, and households.
- `subskills/age-and-income-sensitive-language.md` for age, older adults, youth, income, poverty, homelessness, benefits, fees, debt, and financial hardship.
- `subskills/trauma-informed-language.md` for violence, abuse, disasters, loss, crisis response, behavioral health, grief, and distressing events.
- `subskills/public-safety-and-health-language.md` for emergency notices, public health, law enforcement, courts, corrections, hazards, outbreaks, and safety instructions.

## Standard workflow

1. Identify the content purpose.
   - What must the resident understand or do?
   - What decision, service, risk, or next step is involved?

2. Identify the audience and context.
   - Who is affected?
   - Is the audience seeking help, reporting harm, complying with a requirement, checking eligibility, responding to an emergency, or receiving a denial?

3. Locate sensitive references.
   - Flag references to identity, disability, age, income, health, safety, housing, enforcement, immigration, family status, trauma, or legal status.

4. Test necessity.
   - Ask whether each sensitive descriptor is needed for comprehension, service delivery, eligibility, safety, data accuracy, or legal compliance.
   - Remove descriptors that are not necessary.

5. Rewrite for respect and action.
   - Use direct, plain language.
   - Keep the focus on services, choices, requirements, supports, deadlines, and next steps.
   - Avoid labels, stereotypes, blame, shame, and unnecessarily passive or institutional language.

6. Preserve required terms with explanation.
   - If technical, legal, medical, or program language must remain, define it in plain language near first use.

7. Recommend escalation when needed.
   - Flag content that should be reviewed by legal counsel, privacy staff, HR, public health, emergency management, law enforcement leadership, equity staff, program owners, or communications leadership.

## Output formats

When reviewing content, provide:

- `Overall assessment`: whether the content is generally respectful, risky, unclear, or likely to stigmatize.
- `Priority issues`: the most important language concerns.
- `Recommended rewrite`: revised text that keeps the original meaning.
- `Rationale`: brief explanation of the language choice.
- `Escalation needed`: any legal, privacy, policy, clinical, safety, or emergency review concerns.

When creating content, provide:

- Audience and context assumptions.
- Draft language.
- Optional alternatives for formal, plain, urgent, or community-centered tone.
- Notes about terms that should be verified with the program owner or affected community.

## Do not

- Do not sanitize or weaken critical safety, eligibility, enforcement, or health information.
- Do not erase legally required terms.
- Do not assume identities, pronouns, diagnoses, immigration status, income status, family structure, or lived experience.
- Do not use euphemisms that obscure requirements, risks, or consequences.
- Do not make clinical, legal, HR, or civil rights determinations.
- Do not present inclusive-language edits as a substitute for community review when content materially affects a specific community.
