# Public Safety and Health Language Subskill

## Use when
Content involves public health, emergency response, hazards, evacuations, outbreaks, law enforcement, courts, corrections, code enforcement, inspections, animal control, environmental health, or urgent safety instructions.

## Goals
- Keep urgent information clear, accurate, and actionable.
- Avoid unnecessary fear, stigma, or blame.
- Make responsibilities, risks, deadlines, and next steps explicit.

## Guidance

1. State the action first in urgent contexts.
   - Use direct verbs: `Evacuate`, `Avoid`, `Call`, `Report`, `Boil`, `Stay away`, `Apply`, `Check`, `Register`.
   - Include who must act, where, by when, and how.

2. Avoid stigma in health communications.
   - Do not define people by disease, exposure, vaccination status, disability, substance use, or housing status unless relevant.
   - Explain risk factors without moral judgment.

3. Use neutral enforcement language.
   - Describe requirements, observed conditions, possible consequences, appeal rights, and help available.
   - Avoid language that presumes guilt before findings are made.

4. Be precise with public safety descriptions.
   - Use `suspect`, `person of interest`, `person arrested`, `person cited`, or `person convicted` only when accurate to the stage of the process.
   - Avoid dehumanizing group labels.

5. Pair risk with next steps.
   - Do not only state a hazard. Include protective actions, service contacts, reporting channels, shelter information, or status updates.

6. Preserve official emergency and health terms.
   - Keep official alert levels, evacuation statuses, disease names, incident names, and legal orders when required.
   - Explain technical terms in plain language.

## Preferred rewrites

- `people who may have been exposed` instead of `infected people` when infection is not confirmed.
- `person arrested on suspicion of` instead of `criminal` before conviction.
- `people in custody` instead of `inmates` when a broader, less labeling term is acceptable.
- `follow the evacuation order` instead of `refusing to leave puts everyone at risk`, unless stronger language is required for immediate safety.
- `report unsafe conditions` instead of `report violators` when the focus is hazard reduction.

## Escalate when
- The content concerns emergency orders, evacuation language, disease outbreaks, protected health information, criminal investigations, court matters, deaths, hazardous materials, environmental exposures, or conflicting agency guidance.
