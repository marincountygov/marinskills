# Disability Language Subskill

## Use when
Content refers to disability, access needs, accommodations, assistive technology, functional limitations, neurodiversity, mental health, chronic illness, mobility, hearing, vision, speech, cognition, or disability services.

## Goals
- Use respectful, accurate, and non-ableist language.
- Avoid defining people by impairments, limitations, or services received.
- Preserve clarity about accommodations, rights, access, and service instructions.

## Guidance

1. Use person-first or identity-first language based on context.
   - Default to neutral constructions such as `people with disabilities` when no preference is known.
   - Preserve identity-first language when used by the person or community, such as `Deaf community`, `Autistic people`, or another self-identified term.

2. Avoid ableist metaphors and labels.
   - Replace terms such as `crazy`, `insane`, `lame`, `blind to`, `deaf to`, `crippled by`, `wheelchair-bound`, or `suffers from`.
   - Use precise alternatives: `unreasonable`, `ineffective`, `unaware of`, `does not address`, `person who uses a wheelchair`, `person has`.

3. Focus on access and barriers.
   - Prefer `accessible entrance` over `handicapped entrance`.
   - Prefer `people who need accommodations` over `special needs` unless the term is part of an official program name.
   - Describe the barrier or support, not the person as the problem.

4. Be specific about services and actions.
   - State how to request an accommodation, deadline if any, contact method, and available formats.
   - Avoid vague statements like `reasonable accommodations are available` without next steps.

5. Do not disclose unnecessary disability information.
   - Include disability status only when needed for eligibility, service delivery, accommodation, data reporting, or legal notice.

## Preferred rewrites

- `disabled person` or `person with a disability` depending on stated preference, instead of `the disabled`.
- `person who uses a wheelchair` instead of `wheelchair-bound`.
- `accessible parking` instead of `handicapped parking` unless citing a sign or official title.
- `person with a mental health condition` instead of `mentally ill person` when a condition must be referenced.
- `person receiving services` instead of `client is dependent on services`.

## Escalate when
- The content concerns ADA, FEHA, accommodations, discrimination complaints, service animals, disability benefits, medical verification, or employment action.
- The content includes diagnosis, treatment, mental health crisis, involuntary hold, protected health information, or disability-related records.
