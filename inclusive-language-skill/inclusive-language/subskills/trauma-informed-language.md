# Trauma-Informed Language Subskill

## Use when
Content involves violence, abuse, exploitation, disasters, death, injury, grief, behavioral health crisis, substance use, displacement, harassment, discrimination, victim services, enforcement, or other distressing events.

## Goals
- Reduce unnecessary distress while keeping critical information clear.
- Avoid blame, sensationalism, and graphic detail.
- Support agency, choice, safety, and next steps.

## Guidance

1. Lead with safety and action.
   - Tell people what happened only to the extent needed.
   - State what they can do, where to get help, what services are available, and what happens next.

2. Avoid victim-blaming.
   - Do not imply that a person caused harm by their choices, location, clothing, relationship, housing status, substance use, or reporting delay.
   - Use neutral descriptions of events and impacts.

3. Avoid unnecessary graphic detail.
   - Include detail only when needed for identification, safety, legal notice, or health instructions.
   - Do not use sensational or emotionally amplified wording.

4. Use agency-preserving language.
   - Prefer `person who experienced harm`, `survivor`, `person seeking support`, or the term used by the person or program.
   - Avoid forcing labels such as `victim` or `survivor` unless legally required or contextually appropriate.

5. Be careful with behavioral health and substance use.
   - Use `person with a substance use disorder`, `person seeking treatment`, or `person experiencing a behavioral health crisis` when relevant.
   - Avoid terms such as `addict`, `abuser`, `crazy`, or `committed suicide`.

6. Provide choices where possible.
   - Include optional supports, confidential resources, language access, safety planning, or alternate contact methods when available.

## Preferred rewrites

- `person who died by suicide` instead of `committed suicide`.
- `person who experienced sexual assault` instead of `rape victim` when not legally constrained.
- `people affected by the fire` instead of `fire victims` when broader and less labeling.
- `person with a substance use disorder` instead of `drug abuser`.
- `Report concerns` instead of `Turn in offenders` when public safety goals allow less adversarial wording.

## Escalate when
- The content concerns child abuse, elder abuse, domestic violence, sexual assault, suicide, homicide, disasters, missing persons, behavioral health crisis, mandatory reporting, confidential services, or law enforcement investigations.
