# Conversational Style Sub-skill

Use this sub-skill when content sounds bureaucratic, evasive, legalistic, stiff, or unclear about who must do what.

## Main rules

- Use active voice when it makes the actor clear.
- Use "you" for the reader when appropriate.
- Use "we" for the agency or organization when responsibility matters.
- Use present tense where possible.
- Use examples to clarify abstract rules.
- Use "must" for requirements.
- Avoid "shall" in plain-language instructions unless quoting legal text.

## Active voice pattern

Ask: Who does what?

- Passive: "The form must be submitted by June 1."
- Active: "You must submit the form by June 1."

Use passive voice only when:

- The actor is unknown.
- The actor does not matter.
- The law itself triggers the result and naming an actor would be inaccurate.
- Active voice would wrongly assign responsibility.

## Requirements

Use:

- must = required
- must not = prohibited
- may = permitted
- should = recommended but not required
- will = future action or expected result

Avoid using "shall" as a general-purpose requirement word in public-facing content. Convert it based on meaning:

- "The applicant shall submit..." -> "You must submit..."
- "The department shall review..." -> "We will review..."
- "The permit shall expire..." -> "The permit expires..."

## Tone

Plain language should be direct and respectful. Do not make the text childish, patronizing, or artificially casual. Match the seriousness of the subject.

## Examples

Use examples when:

- A rule is abstract.
- The reader must choose between options.
- A condition or exception is easy to misread.
- A calculation, eligibility rule, or document requirement needs illustration.
