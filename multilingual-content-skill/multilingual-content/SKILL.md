# Multilingual Content Skill

## Purpose
Improve translation readiness, multilingual publishing consistency, glossary control, bilingual layout, and language-access notice quality.

Use this skill when asked to prepare, review, revise, or assess content that will be translated, published in multiple languages, displayed bilingually, or used to support language access for residents.

## When to use this skill
Use this skill for requests involving:

- Preparing English source content for translation
- Reviewing multilingual publishing quality and consistency
- Checking Spanish-language content for clarity, tone, and alignment with the English source
- Identifying terms that need glossary control
- Assessing machine translation risks
- Designing bilingual or multilingual page layouts
- Drafting or reviewing language-access notices
- Deciding when human review, bilingual staff review, or certified translation should be requested

## Operating principles

1. **Source clarity comes first.** Improve the source content before translation. Short, direct, structured source text reduces translation errors.
2. **Meaning beats word-for-word equivalence.** Preserve resident task meaning, eligibility conditions, deadlines, rights, requirements, and next steps.
3. **Use controlled terminology.** Flag program names, legal terms, service names, form titles, department names, and recurring task labels for glossary handling.
4. **Respect language-access risk.** Treat content involving rights, benefits, safety, health, emergencies, legal obligations, deadlines, payments, eligibility, or enforcement as higher risk.
5. **Do not overclaim fluency or certification.** Provide editorial review and risk identification. Do not represent output as certified, legally approved, or a substitute for qualified translation review.
6. **Support resident completion.** Multilingual content should help residents understand what applies to them, what action to take, what documents are needed, when to act, and how to get help.
7. **Preserve plain-language standards.** Keep sentences short, avoid idioms, avoid unexplained acronyms, and avoid agency-centered phrasing.

## Default workflow

When reviewing or creating multilingual content:

1. Identify the content type, audience, language(s), channel, and resident task.
2. Determine risk level:
   - Low: general information, navigation, outreach, non-binding descriptions.
   - Medium: service instructions, forms, applications, payment information, procedural steps.
   - High: legal rights, eligibility, public health, emergencies, enforcement, benefits, deadlines, appeals, privacy, or safety.
3. Improve the source text for translation readiness.
4. Identify controlled terms and glossary candidates.
5. Check whether machine translation is appropriate, risky, or inappropriate without human review.
6. Review layout and publishing needs for bilingual or multilingual display.
7. Add or improve language-access notices where needed.
8. Recommend review routing for high-risk content.

## Expected outputs

Depending on the request, produce one or more of the following:

- Translation-ready source revision
- Translation-readiness checklist
- Spanish review notes and suggested edits
- Glossary table with source term, approved rendering, context, and notes
- Machine-translation risk assessment
- Bilingual layout recommendation
- Language-access notice draft or revision
- Review-routing recommendation

## Quality checklist

Before finalizing, check that:

- The resident task is clear.
- The source text avoids idioms, slang, unexplained acronyms, and long embedded clauses.
- Dates, deadlines, fees, phone numbers, URLs, office names, form names, and eligibility conditions are preserved.
- Required actions are stated as steps.
- Glossary terms are identified and handled consistently.
- High-risk content is flagged for qualified review.
- The output does not claim to be certified translation, legal advice, or final compliance approval.
